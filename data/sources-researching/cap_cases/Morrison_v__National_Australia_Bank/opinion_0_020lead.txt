<opinion type="majority">
<author id="b300-5">Justice Scalia</author>
<p id="AGR">delivered the opinion of the Court.</p>
<p id="b300-6">We decide whether § 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 provides a cause of action to foreign plaintiffs suing <page-number citation-index="1" label="251">*251</page-number>foreign and American defendants for misconduct in connection with securities traded on foreign exchanges.</p>
<p id="b301-5">I</p>
<p id="b301-6">Respondent National Australia Bank Limited (National) was, during the relevant time, the largest bank in Australia. Its Ordinary Shares — what in America would be called “common stock” — are traded on the Australian Stock Exchange Limited and on other foreign securities exchanges, but not on any exchange in the United States. There are listed on the New York Stock Exchange, however, National’s American Depositary Receipts (ADRs), which represent the right to receive a specified number of National’s Ordinary Shares. <span class="citation" data-id="1226239"><a href="/opinion/1226239/morrison-v-national-australia-bank-ltd/#168" aria-description="Citation for case: Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd.">547 F. 3d 167, 168</a></span>, and n. 1 (CA2 2008).</p>
<p id="b301-7">The complaint alleges the following facts, which we accept as true. In February 1998, National bought respondent HomeSide Lending, Inc., a mortgage-servicing company headquartered in Florida. HomeSide’s business was to receive fees for servicing mortgages (essentially the administrative tasks associated with collecting mortgage payments, see J. Rosenberg, Dictionary of Banking and Financial Services 600 (2d ed. 1985)). The rights to receive those fees, so-called mortgage-servicing rights, can provide a valuable income stream. See 2 The New Palgrave Dictionary of Money and Finance 817 (P. Newman, M. Milgate, &amp; J. Eatwell eds. 1992). How valuable each of the rights is depends, in part, on the likelihood that the mortgage to which it applies will be fully repaid before it is due, terminating the need for servicing. HomeSide calculated the present value of its mortgage-servicing rights by using valuation models designed to take this likelihood into account. It recorded the value of its assets, and the numbers appeared in National’s financial statements.</p>
<p id="b301-8">From 1998 until 2001, National’s annual reports and other public documents touted the success of HomeSide’s business, and respondents Frank Cicutto (National’s managing direc<page-number citation-index="1" label="252">*252</page-number>tor and chief executive officer), Kevin Race (HomeSide’s chief operating officer), and Hugh Harris (HomeSide’s chief executive officer) did the same in public statements. But on July 5, 2001, National announced that it was writing down the value of HomeSide’s assets by $450 million; and then again on September 3, by another $1.75 billion. The prices of both Ordinary Shares and ADRs slumped. After downplaying the July writedown, National explained the September writedown as the result of a failure to anticipate the lowering of prevailing interest rates (lower interest rates lead to more refinancings, <em>i. e., </em>more early repayments of mortgages), other mistaken assumptions in the financial models, and the loss of goodwill. According to the complaint, however, HomeSide, Race, Harris, and another Home-Side senior executive who is also a respondent here had manipulated HomeSide’s financial models to make the rates of early repayment unrealistically low in order to cause the mortgage-servicing rights to appear more valuable than they really were. The complaint also alleges that National and Cicutto were aware of this deception by July 2000, but did nothing about it.</p>
<p id="b302-4">As relevant here, petitioners Russell Leslie Owen and Brian and Geraldine Silverlock, all Australians, purchased National’s Ordinary Shares in 2000 and 2001, before the writedowns.<footnotemark>1</footnotemark> They sued National, HomeSide, Cicutto, and the three HomeSide executives in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York for alleged violations of §§ 10(b) and 20(a) of the Securities Exchange Act <page-number citation-index="1" label="253">*253</page-number>of 1934,<span class="citation no-link">48 Stat. 891,15</span> U. S. C. §§ 78j(b) and 78t(a), and Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Rule 10b-5, <span class="citation no-link">17 CFR §240</span>.10b-5 (2009), promulgated pursuant to § 10(b).<footnotemark>2</footnotemark> They sought to represent a class of foreign purchasers of National’s Ordinary Shares during a specified period up to the September writedown. <span class="citation" data-id="1226239"><a href="/opinion/1226239/morrison-v-national-australia-bank-ltd/#169" aria-description="Citation for case: Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd.">547 F. 3d, at 169</a></span>.</p>
<p id="b303-5">Respondents moved to dismiss for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(1) and for failure to state a claim under Rule 12(b)(6). The District Court granted the motion on the former ground, finding no jurisdiction because the acts in this country were, “at most, a link in the chain of an alleged overall securities fraud scheme that culminated abroad.” <em>In re National Australia Bank Securities Litigation, </em>No. 03 Civ. 6537 (BSJ), <span class="citation no-link">2006 WL 3844465</span>, *8 (SDNY, Oct. 25, 2006). The Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit affirmed on similar grounds. The acts performed in the United States did not “compris[e] the heart of the alleged fraud.” <span class="citation" data-id="1226239"><a href="/opinion/1226239/morrison-v-national-australia-bank-ltd/#175" aria-description="Citation for case: Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd.">547 F. 3d, at 175-176</a></span>. We granted certiorari, <span class="citation no-link">558 U. S. 1047</span> (2009).</p>
<p id="b303-6">II</p>
<p id="b303-7">Before addressing the question presented, we must correct a threshold error in the Second Circuit’s analysis. It considered the extraterritorial reach of § 10(b) to raise a question of subject-matter jurisdiction, wherefore it affirmed the Dis<page-number citation-index="1" label="254">*254</page-number>trict Court’s dismissal under Rule 12(b)(1). See <span class="citation" data-id="1226239"><a href="/opinion/1226239/morrison-v-national-australia-bank-ltd/#177" aria-description="Citation for case: Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd.">547 F. 3d, at 177</a></span>. In this regard it was following Circuit precedent, see <em>Schoenbaum </em>v. <em>Firstbrook, </em><span class="citation multiple-matches"><a href="/c/F.%202d/405/200/">405 F. 2d 200</a></span>, 208, modified on other grounds en banc, <span class="citation multiple-matches"><a href="/c/F.%202d/405/215/">405 F. 2d 215</a></span> (1968). The Second Circuit is hardly alone in taking this position, see, <em>e. g., In re CP Ships Ltd. Securities Litigation, </em><span class="citation" data-id="78452"><a href="/opinion/78452/in-re-cp-ships-ltd-securities-litigation/#1313" aria-description="Citation for case: In Re CP Ships Ltd. Securities Litigation">578 F. 3d 1306, 1313</a></span> (CA11 2009); <em>Continental Grain (Australia) Pty. Ltd. </em>v. <em>Pacific Oilseeds, Inc., </em><span class="citation" data-id="363558"><a href="/opinion/363558/continental-grain-australia-pty-ltd-appellant-v-pacific-oilseeds/#421" aria-description="Citation for case: CONTINENTAL GRAIN (AUSTRALIA) PTY. LTD., Appellant, v....">592 F. 2d 409, 421</a></span> (CA8 1979).</p>
<p id="b304-4">But to ask what conduct § 10(b) reaches is to ask what conduct § 10(b) prohibits, which is a merits question. Subject-matter jurisdiction, by contrast, “refers to a tribunal’s ‘ “power to hear a case.” ’ ” <em>Union Pacific R. Co. </em>v. <em>Locomotive Engineers, </em><span class="citation" data-id="1753"><a href="/opinion/1753/union-pacific-r-co-v-locomotive-engineers-and-trainmen-gen-comm-of/#81" aria-description="Citation for case: Union Pacific R. Co. v. Locomotive Engineers and Trainmen...">558 U. S. 67, 81</a></span> (2009) (quoting <em>Arbaugh </em>v. <em>Y &amp; H Corp., </em><span class="citation" data-id="145677"><a href="/opinion/145677/arbaugh-v-y-h-corp/#514" aria-description="Citation for case: Arbaugh v. Y &amp; H Corp.">546 U. S. 500, 514</a></span> (2006), in turn quoting <em>United States </em>v. <em>Cotton, </em><span class="citation" data-id="118505"><a href="/opinion/118505/united-states-v-cotton/#630" aria-description="Citation for case: United States v. Cotton">535 U. S. 625, 630</a></span> (2002)). It presents an issue quite separate from the question whether the allegations the plaintiff makes entitle him to relief. See <em>Bell </em>v. <em>Hood, </em><span class="citation" data-id="9419809"><a href="/opinion/104272/bell-v-hood/#682" aria-description="Citation for case: Bell v. Hood">327 U. S. 678, 682</a></span> (1946). The District Court here had jurisdiction under 15 U. S. C. § 78aa<footnotemark>3</footnotemark> to adjudicate the question whether § 10(b) applies to National’s conduct.</p>
<p id="b304-5">In view of this error, which the parties do not dispute, petitioners ask us to remand. We think that unnecessary. Since nothing in the analysis of the .courts below turned on the mistake, a remand would only require a new Rule 12(b)(6) label for the same Rule 12(b)(1) conclusion. As we have done before in situations like this, see, <em>e. g., Romero </em>v. <em>International Terminal Operating Co., </em><span class="citation" data-id="9421743"><a href="/opinion/105825/romero-v-international-terminal-operating-co/#359" aria-description="Citation for case: Romero v. International Terminal Operating Co.">358 U. S. 354, 359, 381-384</a></span> (1959), we proceed to address whether petitioners’ allegations state a claim.</p>
<p id="b305-4"><page-number citation-index="1" label="255">*255</page-number>III</p>
<p id="b305-5">A</p>
<p id="b305-6">It is a “longstanding principle of American law 'that legislation of Congress, unless a contrary intent appears, is meant to apply only within the territorial jurisdiction of the United States.’ ” <em>EEOC </em>v. <em>Arabian American Oil Co., </em><span class="citation" data-id="9432237"><a href="/opinion/112565/equal-employment-opportunity-commission-v-arabian-american-oil-co/#248" aria-description="Citation for case: Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Arabian...">499 U. S. 244, 248</a></span> (1991) <em>(Aramco) </em>(quoting <em>Foley Bros., Inc. </em>v. <em>Filardo, </em><span class="citation" data-id="104638"><a href="/opinion/104638/foley-bros-inc-v-filardo/#285" aria-description="Citation for case: Foley Bros., Inc. v. Filardo">336 U. S. 281, 285</a></span> (1949)). This principle represents a canon of construction, or a presumption about a statute’s meaning, rather than a limit upon Congress’s power to legislate, see <em>Blackmer </em>v. <em>United States, </em><span class="citation" data-id="101836"><a href="/opinion/101836/blackmer-v-united-states/#437" aria-description="Citation for case: Blackmer v. United States">284 U. S. 421, 437</a></span> (1932). It rests on the perception that Congress ordinarily legislates with respect to domestic, not foreign, matters. <em>Smith </em>v. <em>United States, </em><span class="citation" data-id="9432745"><a href="/opinion/112827/smith-v-united-states/#204" aria-description="Citation for case: Smith v. United States">507 U. S. 197, 204, n. 5</a></span> (1993). Thus, “unless there is the affirmative intention of the Congress clearly expressed” to give a statute extraterritorial effect, “we must presume it is primarily concerned with domestic conditions.” <em>Aramco, supra, </em>at 248 (internal quotation marks omitted). The canon or presumption applies regardless of whether there is a risk of conflict between the American statute and a foreign law, see <em>Sale </em>v. <em>Haitian Centers Council, Inc., </em><span class="citation" data-id="9432858"><a href="/opinion/112892/sale-v-haitian-centers-council-inc/#173" aria-description="Citation for case: Sale v. Haitian Centers Council, Inc.">509 U. S. 155, 173-174</a></span> (1993). When a statute gives no clear indication of an extraterritorial application, it has none.</p>
<p id="b305-7">Despite this principle of interpretation, long and often recited in our opinions, the Second Circuit believed that, because the Exchange Act is silent as to the extraterritorial application of § 10(b), it was left to the court to “discern” whether Congress would have wanted the statute to apply. See <span class="citation" data-id="1226239"><a href="/opinion/1226239/morrison-v-national-australia-bank-ltd/#170" aria-description="Citation for case: Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd.">547 F. 3d, at 170</a></span> (internal quotation marks omitted). This disregard of the presumption against extraterritoriality did not originate with the Court of Appeals panel in this case. It has been repeated over many decades by various Courts of Appeals in determining the application of the Exchange Act, and § 10(b) in particular, to fraudulent schemes that involve conduct and effects abroad. That has produced <page-number citation-index="1" label="256">*256</page-number>a collection of tests for divining what Congress would have wanted, complex in formulation and unpredictable in application.</p>
<p id="b306-4">As of 1967, District Courts at least in the Southern District of New York had consistently concluded that, by reason of the presumption against extraterritoriality, § 10(b) did not apply when the stock transactions underlying the violation occurred abroad. See <em>Schoenbaum </em>v. <em>Firstbrook, </em><span class="citation" data-id="1481115"><a href="/opinion/1481115/schoenbaum-v-firstbrook/#392" aria-description="Citation for case: Schoenbaum v. Firstbrook">268 F. Supp. 385, 392</a></span> (1967) (citing <em>Ferraioli </em>v. <em>Cantor, </em>CCH <span class="citation no-link">Fed. Sec. L. Rep. ¶ 91615</span> (SDNY 1965), and <em>Kook </em>v. <em>Crang, </em><span class="citation" data-id="1902998"><a href="/opinion/1902998/kook-v-crang/#390" aria-description="Citation for case: Kook v. Crang">182 F. Supp. 388, 390</a></span> (SDNY I960)). <em>Schoenbaum </em>involved the sale in Canada of the treasury shares of a Canadian corporation whose publicly traded shares (but not, of course, its treasury shares) were listed on both the American Stock Exchange and the Toronto Stock Exchange. Invoking the presumption against extraterritoriality, the court held that § 10(b) was inapplicable (though it incorrectly viewed the defect as jurisdictional). <span class="citation" data-id="1481115"><a href="/opinion/1481115/schoenbaum-v-firstbrook/#391" aria-description="Citation for case: Schoenbaum v. Firstbrook">268 F. Supp., at 391-392, 393-394</a></span>. The decision in <em>Schoenbaum </em>was reversed, however, by a Second Circuit opinion which held that “neither the usual presumption against extraterritorial application of legislation nor the specific language of [§] 30(b) show Congressional intent to preclude application of the Exchange Act to transactions regarding stocks traded in the United States which are effected outside the United States . . . .” <em>Schoenbaum, </em>405 F. 2d, at 206. It sufficed to apply § 10(b) that, although the transactions in treasury shares took place in Canada, they affected the value of the common shares publicly traded in the United States. See <em>id., </em>at 208-209. Application of § 10(b), the Second Circuit found, was “necessary to protect American investors,” <em>id., </em>at 206.</p>
<p id="b306-5">The Second Circuit took another step with <em>Leasco Data Processing Equip. Corp. </em>v. <em>Maxwell, </em><span class="citation multiple-matches"><a href="/c/F.%202d/468/1326/">468 F. 2d 1326</a></span> (1972), which involved an American company that had been fraudulently induced to buy securities in England. There, unlike in <em>Schoenbaum, </em>some of the deceptive conduct had occurred <page-number citation-index="1" label="257">*257</page-number>in the United States but the corporation whose securities were traded (abroad) was not listed on any domestic exchange. <em>Leasco </em>said that the presumption against extraterritoriality applies only to matters over which the United States would not have prescriptive jurisdiction, 468 F. 2d, at 1334. Congress had prescriptive jurisdiction to regulate the deceptive conduct in this country, the language of the Act could be read to cover that conduct, and the court concluded that “if Congress had thought about the point,” it would have wanted § 10(b) to apply. <em>Id., </em>at 1334-1337.</p>
<p id="b307-5">With <em>Schoenbaum </em>and <em>Leasco </em>on the books, the Second Circuit had excised the presumption against extraterritoriality from the jurisprudence of § 10(b) and replaced it with the inquiry whether it would be reasonable (and hence what Congress would have wanted) to apply the statute to a given situation. As long as there was prescriptive jurisdiction to regulate, the Second Circuit explained, whether to apply § 10(b) even to “predominantly foreign” transactions became a matter of whether a court thought Congress “wished the precious resources of United States courts and law enforcement agencies to be devoted to them rather than leave the problem to foreign countries.” <em>Bersch </em>v. <em>Drexel Firestone, Inc., </em><span class="citation" data-id="8896986"><a href="/opinion/8909383/bersch-v-drexel-firestone-inc/#985" aria-description="Citation for case: Bersch v. Drexel Firestone, Inc.">519 F. 2d 974, 985</a></span> (1975); see also <em>IIT </em>v. <em>Vencap, Ltd., </em><span class="citation" data-id="8896988"><a href="/opinion/8909385/iit-v-vencap-ltd/#1017" aria-description="Citation for case: IIT v. Vencap, Ltd.">519 F. 2d 1001, 1017-1018</a></span> (CA2 1975).</p>
<p id="b307-6">The Second Circuit had thus established that application of § 10(b) could be premised upon either some effect on American securities markets or investors <em>(Schoenbaum) </em>or significant conduct in the United States <em>(Leasco). </em>It later formalized these two applications into (1) an “effects test,” “whether the wrongful conduct had a substantial effect in the United States or upon United States citizens,” and (2) a “conduct test,” “whether the wrongful conduct occurred in the United States.” <em>SEC </em>v. <em>Berger, </em><span class="citation" data-id="781166"><a href="/opinion/781166/securities-and-exchange-commission-v-michael-w-berger-manhattan/#192" aria-description="Citation for case: Securities and Exchange Commission v. Michael W. Berger,...">322 F. 3d 187, 192-193</a></span> (CA2 2003). These became the north star of the Second Circuit's § 10(b) jurisprudence, pointing the way to what Con<page-number citation-index="1" label="258">*258</page-number>gress would have wished. Indeed, the Second Circuit declined to keep its two tests distinct on the ground that “an admixture or combination of the two often gives a better picture of whether there is sufficient United States involvement to justify the exercise of jurisdiction by an American court.” <em>Itoba Ltd. </em>v. <em>Lep Group PLC, </em><span class="citation" data-id="695526"><a href="/opinion/695526/fed-sec-l-rep-p-98815-itoba-limited-v-lep-group-plc-william-r/#122" aria-description="Citation for case: Fed. Sec. L. Rep. P 98,815 Itoba Limited v. Lep Group...">54 F. 3d 118, 122</a></span> (1995). The Second Circuit never put forward a textual or even extratextual basis for these tests. As early as <em><span class="citation" data-id="8896986"><a href="/opinion/8909383/bersch-v-drexel-firestone-inc/" aria-description="Citation for case: Bersch v. Drexel Firestone, Inc.">Bersch</a></span>, </em>it confessed that “if we were asked to point to language in the statutes, or even in the legislative history, that compelled these conclusions, we would be unable to respond,” 519 F. 2d, at 993.</p>
<p id="b308-4">As they developed, these tests were not easy to administer. The conduct test was held to apply differently depending on whether the harmed investors were Americans or foreigners: When the alleged damages consisted of losses to American investors abroad, it was enough that acts “of material importance” performed in the United States “significantly contributed” to that result; whereas those acts must have “directly caused” the result when losses to foreigners abroad were at issue. See <em>ibid. </em>And “merely preparatory activities in the United States” did not suffice “to trigger application of the securities laws for injury to foreigners located abroad.” <em>Id., </em>at 992. This required the court to distinguish between mere preparation and using the United States as a “base” for fraudulent activities in other countries. <span class="citation" data-id="8896988"><a href="/opinion/8909385/iit-v-vencap-ltd/#1017" aria-description="Citation for case: IIT v. Vencap, Ltd."><em>Vencap, supra, </em>at 1017-1018</a></span>. But merely satisfying the conduct test was sometimes insufficient without “‘some additional factor tipping the scales’ ” in favor of the application of American law. <em>Interbrew </em>v. <em>Edperbrascan Corp., </em><span class="citation" data-id="2297860"><a href="/opinion/2297860/interbrew-sa-v-edperbrascan-corp/#432" aria-description="Citation for case: Interbrew S.A. v. Edperbrascan Corp.">23 F. Supp. 2d 425, 432</a></span> (SDNY 1998) (quoting <em>Europe &amp; Overseas Commodity Traders, S. A. </em>v. <em>Banque Paribas London, </em><span class="citation" data-id="6970194"><a href="/opinion/7065996/europe-overseas-commodity-traders-sa-v-banque-paribas-london/#129" aria-description="Citation for case: Europe &amp; Overseas Commodity Traders, S.A. v. Banque...">147 F. 3d 118, 129</a></span> (CA2 1998)). District Courts have noted the difficulty of applying such vague formulations. See, <em>e. g., In re Alstom SA, </em><span class="citation" data-id="2323318"><a href="/opinion/2323318/in-re-alstom-sa-securities-litigation/#366" aria-description="Citation for case: In Re Alstom SA Securities Litigation">406 F. Supp. 2d 346, 366-385</a></span> (SDNY 2005). There is no more damning indictment of the “con<page-number citation-index="1" label="259">*259</page-number>duct” and “effects” tests than the Second Circuit’s own declaration that “the presence or absence of any single factor which was considered significant in other cases ... is not necessarily dispositive in future cases.” <em>IIT </em>v. <em>Cornfeld, </em><span class="citation" data-id="8911259"><a href="/opinion/8922297/iit-v-cornfeld/#918" aria-description="Citation for case: IIT v. Cornfeld">619 F. 2d 909, 918</a></span> (1980) (internal quotation marks omitted).</p>
<p id="b309-5">Other Circuits embraced the Second Circuit’s approach, though not its precise application. Like the Second Circuit, they described their decisions regarding the extraterritorial application of § 10(b) as essentially resolving matters of policy. See, <em>e. g., SEC </em>v. <em>Kasser, </em><span class="citation" data-id="342299"><a href="/opinion/342299/securities-and-exchange-commission-appellant-v-alexander-kasser-et-al/#116" aria-description="Citation for case: SECURITIES AND EXCHANGE COMMISSION, Appellant, v....">548 F. 2d 109,116</a></span> (CA3 1977); <em>Continental Grain, </em><span class="citation" data-id="363558"><a href="/opinion/363558/continental-grain-australia-pty-ltd-appellant-v-pacific-oilseeds/#421" aria-description="Citation for case: CONTINENTAL GRAIN (AUSTRALIA) PTY. LTD., Appellant, v....">592 F. 2d, at 421-422</a></span>; <em>Grunenthal GmbH </em>v. <em>Hotz, </em><span class="citation" data-id="8917354"><a href="/opinion/8927469/grunenthal-gmbh-v-hotz/#424" aria-description="Citation for case: Grunenthal Gmbh v. Hotz">712 F. 2d 421, 424-425</a></span> (CA9 1983); <em>Kauthar SDN BHD </em>v. <em>Sternberg, </em><span class="citation" data-id="6971642"><a href="/opinion/7067393/kauthar-sdn-bhd-v-sternberg/#667" aria-description="Citation for case: Kauthar SDN BHD v. Sternberg">149 F. 3d 659, 667</a></span> (CA7 1998). While applying the same fundamental methodology of balancing interests and arriving at what seemed the best policy, they produced a proliferation of vaguely related variations on the “conduct” and “effects” tests. As described in a leading Seventh Circuit opinion: “Although the circuits . . . seem to agree that there are some transnational situations to which the antifraud provisions of the securities laws are applicable, agreement appears to end at that point.”<footnotemark>4</footnotemark> <span class="citation" data-id="6971642"><a href="/opinion/7067393/kauthar-sdn-bhd-v-sternberg/#665" aria-description="Citation for case: Kauthar SDN BHD v. Sternberg"><em>Id., </em>at 665</a></span>. See <page-number citation-index="1" label="260">*260</page-number>also <span class="citation" data-id="6971642"><a href="/opinion/7067393/kauthar-sdn-bhd-v-sternberg/#665" aria-description="Citation for case: Kauthar SDN BHD v. Sternberg"><em>id., </em>at 665-667</a></span> (describing the approaches of the various Circuits and adopting yet another variation).</p>
<p id="b310-4">At least one Court of Appeals has criticized this line of cases and the interpretive assumption that underlies it. In <em>Zoelsch </em>v. <em>Arthur Andersen &amp; Co., </em><span class="citation" data-id="9476463"><a href="/opinion/491629/klaus-zoelsch-v-arthur-andersen-co/#32" aria-description="Citation for case: Klaus Zoelsch v. Arthur Andersen &amp; Co">824 F. 2d 27, 32</a></span> (1987) (Bork, J.), the District of Columbia Circuit observed that rather than courts’ “divining what ‘Congress would have wished’ if it had addressed the problem[, a] more natural inquiry might be what jurisdiction Congress in fact thought about and conferred.” Although tempted to apply the presumption against extraterritoriality and be done with it, see <span class="citation" data-id="9476463"><a href="/opinion/491629/klaus-zoelsch-v-arthur-andersen-co/#31" aria-description="Citation for case: Klaus Zoelsch v. Arthur Andersen &amp; Co"><em>id., </em>at 31-32</a></span>, that court deferred to the Second Circuit because of its “preeminence in the field of securities law,” <span class="citation" data-id="9476463"><a href="/opinion/491629/klaus-zoelsch-v-arthur-andersen-co/#32" aria-description="Citation for case: Klaus Zoelsch v. Arthur Andersen &amp; Co"><em>id., </em>at 32</a></span>. See also <em>Robinson </em>v. <em>TCI/US West Communications Inc., </em><span class="citation" data-id="743089"><a href="/opinion/743089/robinson-v-tcius-west-communications-inc/#906" aria-description="Citation for case: Robinson v. TCI/US West Communications Inc.">117 F. 3d 900, 906-907</a></span> (CA5 1997) (expressing agreement with Zoelsch’s criticism of the emphasis on policy considerations in some of the cases).</p>
<p id="b310-5">Commentators have criticized the unpredictable and inconsistent application of § 10(b) to transnational cases. See, <em>e. g., </em>Choi &amp; Silberman, Transnational Litigation and Global Securities Class-Action Lawsuits, <span class="citation no-link">2009 Wis. L. Rev. 465</span>,467-468; Chang, Multinational Enforcement of U. S. Securities Laws: The Need for the Clear and Restrained Scope of Extraterritorial Subject-Matter Jurisdiction, 9 Ford. J. Corp. &amp; Fin. L. 89,106-108,115-116 (2004); Langevoort, <em>Schoenbaum </em>Revisited: Limiting the Scope of Antifraud Protection in an Internationalized Securities Marketplace, 55 Law &amp; Con-temp. Prob. 241, 244-248 (1992). Some have challenged the premise underlying the Courts of Appeals’ approach, namely, that Congress did not consider the extraterritorial application of § 10(b) (thereby leaving it open to the courts, supposedly, to determine what Congress would have wanted). See, <em>e. g., </em>Sachs, The International Reach of Rule 10b-5: The Myth of Congressional Silence, 28 Colum. J. Transnat’l L. 677 (1990) (arguing that Congress considered, but rejected, applying the Exchange Act to transactions abroad). Others, <page-number citation-index="1" label="261">*261</page-number>more fundamentally, have noted that using congressional silence as a justification for judge-made rules violates the traditional principle that silence means no extraterritorial application. See, <em>e. g., </em>Note, Let There Be Fraud (Abroad): A Proposal for a New U. S. Jurisprudence with Regard to the Extraterritorial Application of the Anti-Fraud Provisions of the 1933 and 1934 Securities Acts, 28 Law <em>&amp; </em>Pol’y Int’l Bus. 477, 492-493 (1997).</p>
<p id="b311-5">The criticisms seem to us justified. The results of judicial-speculation-made-law — divining what Congress would have wanted if it had thought of the situation before the court — demonstrate the wisdom of the presumption against extraterritoriality. Rather than guess anew in each case, we apply the presumption in all cases, preserving a stable background against which Congress can legislate with predictable effects.<footnotemark>5</footnotemark></p>
<p id="b311-6">B</p>
<p id="b311-7">Rule 10b-5, the regulation under which petitioners have brought suit,<footnotemark>6</footnotemark> was promulgated under § 10(b), and “does not <page-number citation-index="1" label="262">*262</page-number>extend beyond conduct encompassed by §10(b)’s prohibition.” <em>United States </em>v. <em>O’Hagan, </em><span class="citation" data-id="9433519"><a href="/opinion/118143/united-states-v-ohagan/#651" aria-description="Citation for case: United States v. O&#x27;Hagan">521 U. S. 642, 651</a></span> (1997). Therefore, if § 10(b) is not extraterritorial, neither is Rule 10b-5.</p>
<p id="b312-4">On its face, § 10(b) contains nothing to suggest it applies abroad:</p>
<blockquote id="b312-5">“It shall be unlawful for any person, directly or indirectly, by the use of any means or instrumentality of interstate commerce or of the mails, or of any facility of any national securities exchange—</blockquote>
<blockquote id="b312-6">“[t]o use or employ, in connection with the purchase or sale of any security registered on a national securities exchange or any security not so registered,. . . any manipulative or deceptive device or contrivance in contravention of such rules and regulations as the [Securities and Exchange] Commission may prescribe . . . .” 15 U. S. C. 78j(b).</blockquote>
<p id="b312-7">Petitioners and the Solicitor General contend, however, that three things indicate that § 10(b) or the Exchange Act in general has at least some extraterritorial application.</p>
<p id="b312-8">First, they point to the definition of “interstate commerce,” a term used in § 10(b), which includes “trade, commerce, transportation, or communication . . . between any foreign country and any State.” 15 U. S. C. §78c(a)(17). But “we have repeatedly held that even statutes that contain <page-number citation-index="1" label="263">*263</page-number>broad language in their definitions of bommeree’ that expressly refer to <em>‘foreign </em>commerce’ do not apply abroad.” <em>Aramco, </em><span class="citation" data-id="9432237"><a href="/opinion/112565/equal-employment-opportunity-commission-v-arabian-american-oil-co/#251" aria-description="Citation for case: Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Arabian...">499 U. S., at 251</a></span>; see <span class="citation" data-id="9432237"><a href="/opinion/112565/equal-employment-opportunity-commission-v-arabian-american-oil-co/#251" aria-description="Citation for case: Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Arabian..."><em>id., </em>at 251-252</a></span> (discussing cases). The general reference to foreign commerce in the definition of “interstate commerce” does not defeat the presumption against extraterritoriality.<footnotemark>7</footnotemark></p>
<p id="b313-5">Petitioners and the Solicitor General next point out that Congress, in describing the purposes of the Exchange Act, observed that the “prices established and offered in such transactions are generally disseminated and quoted throughout the United States and foreign countries.” 15 U. S. C. § 78b(2). The antecedent of “such transactions,” however, is found in the first sentence of the section, which declares that “transactions in securities as commonly conducted upon securities exchanges and over-the-counter markets are affected with a national public interest.” §78b. Nothing suggests that this <em>national </em>public interest pertains to transactions conducted upon <em>foreign </em>exchanges and markets. The fleeting reference to the dissemination and quotation abroad of the prices of securities traded in domestic exchanges and markets cannot overcome the presumption against extraterritoriality.</p>
<p id="b313-6">Finally, there is § 30(b) of the Exchange Act, 15 U. S. C. §78dd(b), which <em>does </em>mention the Act’s extraterritorial application: “The provisions of [the Exchange Act] or of any rule or regulation thereunder shall not apply to any person insofar as he transacts a business in securities without the <page-number citation-index="1" label="264">*264</page-number>jurisdiction of the United States,” unless he does so in violation of regulations promulgated by the Securities and Exchange Commission “to prevent . . . evasion of [the Act].” (The parties have pointed us to no regulation promulgated pursuant to § 30(b).) The Solicitor General argues that “[this] exemption would have no function if the Act did not apply in the first instance to securities transactions that occur abroad.” Brief for United States as <em>Amicus Curiae </em>14.</p>
<p id="b314-4">We are not convinced. In the first place, it would be odd for Congress to indicate the extraterritorial application of the whole Exchange Act by means of a provision imposing a condition precedent to its application abroad. And if the whole Act applied abroad, why would the Commission’s enabling regulations be limited to those preventing “evasion” of the Act, rather than all those preventing “violation”? The provision seems to us directed at actions abroad that might conceal a domestic violation, or might cause what would otherwise be a domestic violation to escape on a technicality. At most, the Solicitor General’s proposed inference is possible; but possible interpretations of statutory language do not override the presumption against extraterritoriality. See <em>Aramco, supra, </em>at 253.</p>
<p id="b314-5">The Solicitor General also fails to account for § 30(a), which reads in relevant part as follows:</p>
<blockquote id="b314-6">“It shall be unlawful for any broker or dealer ... to make use of the mails or of any means or instrumentality of interstate commerce for the purpose of effecting on an exchange not within or subject to the jurisdiction of the United States, any transaction in any security the issuer of which is a resident of, or is organized under the laws of, or has its principal place of business in, a place within or subject to the jurisdiction of the United States, in contravention of such rules and regulations as the Commission may prescribe . . . .” 15 U. S. C. § 78dd(a).</blockquote>
<p id="AGC"><page-number citation-index="1" label="265">*265</page-number>Subsection 30(a) contains what § 10(b) lacks: a clear statement of extraterritorial effect. Its explicit provision for a specific extraterritorial application would be quite superfluous if the rest of the Exchange Act already applied to transactions on foreign exchanges — and its limitation of that application to securities of domestic issuers would be inoperative. Even if that were not true, when a statute provides for some extraterritorial application, the presumption against extraterritoriality operates to limit that provision to its terms. See <em>Microsoft Corp. </em>v. <em>AT&amp;T Corp., </em><span class="citation" data-id="9435074"><a href="/opinion/145735/microsoft-corp-v-att-corp/#455" aria-description="Citation for case: Microsoft Corp. v. At&amp;t Corp.">550 U. S. 437, 455-456</a></span> (2007). No one claims that § 30(a) applies here.</p>
<p id="b315-5">The concurrence claims we have impermissibly narrowed the inquiry in evaluating whether a statute applies abroad, citing for that point the dissent in <em>Aramco, </em>see <em>post, </em>at 278-279. But we do not say, as the concurrence seems to think, that the presumption against extraterritoriality is a “clear statement rule,” <em>post, </em>at 278, if by that is meant a requirement that a statute say “this law applies abroad.” Assuredly context can be consulted as well. But whatever sources of statutory meaning one consults to give “the most faithful reading” of the text, <em>post, </em>at 280, there is no clear indication of extraterritoriality here. The concurrence does not even try to refute that conclusion, but merely puts forward the same (at best) uncertain indications relied upon by petitioners and the Solicitor General. As the opinion <em>for the Court </em>in <em>Aramco </em>(which we prefer to the dissent) shows, those uncertain indications do not suffice.<footnotemark>8</footnotemark></p>
<p id="b315-6">In short, there is no affirmative indication in the Exchange Act that § 10(b) applies extraterritorially, and we therefore conclude that it does not.</p>
<p id="b316-3"><page-number citation-index="1" label="266">*266</page-number>IV</p>
<p id="b316-4">A</p>
<p id="b316-5">Petitioners argue that the conclusion that § 10(b) does not apply extraterritorially does not resolve this case. They contend that they seek no more than domestic application anyway, since Florida is where HomeSide and its senior executives engaged in the deceptive conduct of manipulating HomeSide’s financial models; their complaint also alleged that Race and Hughes made misleading public statements there. This is less an answer to the presumption against extraterritorial application than it is an assertion — a quite valid assertion — that that presumption here (as often) is not self-evidently dispositive, but its application requires further analysis. For it is a rare case of prohibited extraterritorial application that lacks <em>all </em>contact with the territory of the United States. But the presumption against extraterritorial application would be a craven watchdog indeed if it retreated to its kennel whenever <em>some </em>domestic activity is involved in the case. The concurrence seems to imagine just such a timid sentinel, see <em>post, </em>at 280-281, but our cases are to the contrary. In <em>Aramco, </em>for example, the Title VII plaintiff had been hired in Houston, and was an American citizen. See <span class="citation" data-id="9432237"><a href="/opinion/112565/equal-employment-opportunity-commission-v-arabian-american-oil-co/#247" aria-description="Citation for case: Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Arabian...">499 U. S., at 247</a></span>. The Court concluded, however, that neither that territorial event nor that relationship was the “focus” of congressional concern, <span class="citation" data-id="9432237"><a href="/opinion/112565/equal-employment-opportunity-commission-v-arabian-american-oil-co/#255" aria-description="Citation for case: Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Arabian..."><em>id., </em>at 255</a></span>, but rather domestic employment. See also <em>Foley Bros., </em><span class="citation" data-id="104638"><a href="/opinion/104638/foley-bros-inc-v-filardo/#283" aria-description="Citation for case: Foley Bros., Inc. v. Filardo">336 U. S., at 283, 285-286</a></span>.</p>
<p id="b316-6">Applying the same mode of analysis here, we think that the focus of the Exchange Act is not upon the place where the deception originated, but upon purchases and sales of securities in the United States. Section 10(b) does not punish deceptive conduct, but only deceptive conduct “in connection with the purchase or sale of any security registered on a national securities exchange or any security not so registered.” 15 U. S. C. § 78j(b). See <em>SEC </em>v. <em>Zandford, </em><span class="citation" data-id="118513"><a href="/opinion/118513/securities-exchange-commission-v-zandford/#820" aria-description="Citation for case: Securities &amp; Exchange Commission v. Zandford">535 U. S. <page-number citation-index="1" label="267">*267</page-number>813, 820</a></span> (2002). Those purehase-and-sale transactions are the objects of the statute’s solicitude. It is those transactions that the statute seeks to “regulate,” see <em>Superintendent of Ins. of N. Y. </em>v. <em>Bankers Life &amp; Casualty Co., </em><span class="citation" data-id="108391"><a href="/opinion/108391/superintendent-of-insurance-of-new-york-v-bankers-life-casualty-co/#12" aria-description="Citation for case: Superintendent of Insurance of New York v. Bankers Life &amp;...">404 U. S. 6,12</a></span> (1971); it is parties or prospective parties to those transactions that the statute seeks to “protee[t],” <span class="citation" data-id="108391"><a href="/opinion/108391/superintendent-of-insurance-of-new-york-v-bankers-life-casualty-co/#10" aria-description="Citation for case: Superintendent of Insurance of New York v. Bankers Life &amp;..."><em>id., </em>at 10</a></span>. See also <em>Ernst &amp; Ernst </em>v. <em>Hochfelder, </em><span class="citation" data-id="9426352"><a href="/opinion/109420/ernst-ernst-v-hochfelder/#195" aria-description="Citation for case: Ernst &amp; Ernst v. Hochfelder">425 U. S. 185, 195</a></span> (1976). And it is in our view only transactions in securities listed on domestic exchanges, and domestic transactions in other securities, to which § 10(b) applies.<footnotemark>9</footnotemark></p>
<p id="b317-5">The primacy of the domestic exchange is suggested by the very prologue of the Exchange Act, which sets forth as its object “[t]o provide for the regulation of securities exchanges . . . operating in interstate and foreign commerce and-through the mails, to prevent inequitable and unfair practices on such exchanges . . . .” <span class="citation no-link">48 Stat. 881</span>. We know of no one who thought that the Act was intended to “regu<em>lat[e]” foreign </em>securities exchanges — or indeed who even believed that under established principles of international law Congress had the power to do so. The Act’s registration requirements apply only to securities listed on national securities exchanges. 15 U. S. C. § 78£(a).</p>
<p id="b318-3"><page-number citation-index="1" label="268">*268</page-number>With regard to securities <em>not </em>registered on domestic exchanges, the exclusive focus on <em>domestic </em>purchases and sales<footnotemark>10</footnotemark> is strongly confirmed by § 30(a) and (b), discussed earlier. The former extends the normal scope of the Exchange Act’s prohibitions to acts effecting, in violation of rules prescribed by the Commission, a “transaction” in a United States security “on an exchange not within or subject to the jurisdiction of the United States.” §78dd(a). And the latter specifies that the Act does not apply to “any person insofar as he transacts a business in securities without the jurisdiction of the United States,” unless he does so in violation of regulations promulgated by the Commission “to prevent the evasion [of the Act].” § 78dd(b). Under both provisions it is the foreign location of the <em>transaction </em>that establishes (or reflects the presumption of) the Act’s inapplicability, absent regulations by the Commission.</p>
<p id="b318-4">The same focus on domestic transactions is evident in the Securities Act of 1933, <span class="citation no-link">48 Stat. 74</span>, enacted by the same Congress as the Exchange Act, and forming part of the same comprehensive regulation of securities trading. See <em>Central Bank of Denver, N. A. </em>v. <em>First Interstate Bank of Denver, N. A., </em><span class="citation" data-id="9432983"><a href="/opinion/117837/central-bank-of-denver-n-a-v-first-interstate-bank-of-denver-n-a/#170" aria-description="Citation for case: Central Bank of Denver, N. A. v. First Interstate Bank of...">511 U. S. 164, 170-171</a></span> (1994). That legislation makes it unlawful to sell a security, through a prospectus or otherwise, making use of “any means or instruments of transportation or communication in interstate commerce or of the mails,” unless a registration statement is in effect. <page-number citation-index="1" label="269">*269</page-number>15 U. S. C. § 77e(a)(l). The Commission has interpreted that requirement “not to include . . . sales that occur outside the United States.” <span class="citation no-link">17 CFR § 230.901</span> (2009).</p>
<p id="b319-5">Finally, we reject the notion that the Exchange Act reaches conduct in this country affecting exchanges or transactions abroad for the same reason that <em>Aramco </em>rejected overseas application of Title YII to all domestically concluded employment contracts or all employment contracts with American employers: The probability of incompatibility with the applicable laws of other countries is so obvious that if Congress intended such foreign application “it would have addressed the subject of conflicts with foreign laws and procedures.” <span class="citation" data-id="9432237"><a href="/opinion/112565/equal-employment-opportunity-commission-v-arabian-american-oil-co/#256" aria-description="Citation for case: Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Arabian...">499 U. S., at 256</a></span>. Like the United States, foreign countries regulate their domestic securities exchanges and securities transactions occurring within their territorial jurisdiction. And the regulation of other countries often differs from ours as to what constitutes fraud, what disclosures must be made, what damages are recoverable, what discovery is available in litigation, what individual actions may be joined in a single suit, what attorney’s fees are recoverable, and many other matters. See, <em>e. g., </em>Brief for United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland as <em>Amicus Curiae </em>16-21. The Commonwealth of Australia, the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland, and the Republic of France have filed <em>amicus </em>briefs in this case. So have (separately or jointly) such international and foreign organizations as the International Chamber of Commerce, the Swiss Bankers Association, the Federation of German Industries, the French Business Confederation, the Institute of International Bankers, the European Banking Federation, the Australian Bankers’ Association, and the Association Frangaise des Entreprises Privées. They all complain of the interference with foreign securities regulation that application of § 10(b) abroad would produce, and urge the adoption of a clear test that will avoid that consequence. The transactional test we have adopted — whether the purchase or sale <page-number citation-index="1" label="270">*270</page-number>is made in the United States, or involves a security listed on a domestic exchange — meets that requirement.</p>
<p id="b320-4">B</p>
<p id="b320-5">The Solicitor General suggests a different test, which petitioners also endorse: “[A] transnational securities fraud violates [§] 10(b) when the fraud involves significant conduct in the United States that is material to the fraud’s success.” Brief for United States as <em>Amicus Curiae </em>16; see Brief for Petitioners 26. Neither the Solicitor General nor petitioners provide any textual support for this test. The Solicitor General sets forth a number of purposes such a test would serve: achieving a high standard of business ethics in the securities industry, ensuring honest securities markets and thereby promoting investor confidence, and preventing the United States from becoming a “Barbary Coast” for malefactors perpetrating frauds in foreign markets. Brief for United States as <em>Amicus Curiae </em>16-17. But it provides no textual support for the last of these purposes, or for the first two as applied to the foreign securities industry and securities markets abroad. It is our function to give the statute the effect its language suggests, however modest that may be; not to extend it to admirable purposes it might be used to achieve.</p>
<p id="b320-6">If, moreover, one is to be attracted by the desirable consequences of the “significant and material conduct” test, one should also be repulsed by its adverse consequences. While there is no reason to believe that the United States has become the Barbary Coast for those perpetrating frauds on foreign securities markets, some fear that it has become the Shangri-La of class-action litigation for lawyers representing those allegedly cheated in foreign securities markets. See Brief for Infineon Technologies AG as <em>Amicus Curiae </em>1-2, 22-25; Brief for European Aeronautic Defence &amp; Space Co. N. V. et al. as <em>Amici Curiae </em>2-4; Brief for Securities Industry and Financial Markets Association et al. as <em>Amici Curiae </em><page-number citation-index="1" label="271">*271</page-number>10-16; Coffee, Securities Policeman to the World? The Cost of Global Class Actions, N. Y. L. J. 5 (2008); S. Grant &amp; D. Zilka, The Current Role of Foreign Investors in Federal Securities Class Actions, PLI Corporate Law and Practice Handbook Series, PLI Order No. 11072, pp. 15-16 (Sept.-Oct. 2007); Buxbaum, Multinational Class Actions Under Federal Securities Law: Managing Jurisdictional Conflict, 46 Colum. J. Transnat’l L. 14, 38-41 (2007).</p>
<p id="b321-5">As case support for the “significant and material conduct” test, the Solicitor General relies primarily on <em>Pasquantino </em>v. <em>United States, </em><span class="citation" data-id="9434781"><a href="/opinion/142889/pasquantino-v-united-states/" aria-description="Citation for case: Pasquantino v. United States">544 U. S. 349</a></span> (2005).<footnotemark>11</footnotemark> In that case we concluded that the wire-fraud statute, <span class="citation no-link">18 U. S. C. § 1343</span> (2000 ed., Supp. II), was violated by defendants who ordered liquor over the phone from a store in Maryland with the intent to -smuggle it into Canada and deprive the Canadian Government of revenue. <span class="citation" data-id="9434781"><a href="/opinion/142889/pasquantino-v-united-states/#353" aria-description="Citation for case: Pasquantino v. United States">544 U. S., at 353, 371</a></span>. Section 1343 prohibits “any scheme or artifice to defraud,” — fraud <em>simplic</em><page-number citation-index="1" label="272">*272</page-number><em>iter, </em>without any requirement that it be “in connection with” any particular transaction or event. The <em><span class="citation" data-id="9434781"><a href="/opinion/142889/pasquantino-v-united-states/" aria-description="Citation for case: Pasquantino v. United States">Pasquantino</a></span> </em>Court said that the petitioners’ “offense was complete the moment they executed the scheme inside the United States,” and that it was “[t]his domestic element of petitioners’ conduct [that] the Government is punishing.” <span class="citation" data-id="9434781"><a href="/opinion/142889/pasquantino-v-united-states/#371" aria-description="Citation for case: Pasquantino v. United States"><em>Id., </em>at 371</a></span>. Section 10(b), by contrast, punishes not all acts of deception, but only such acts “in connection with the purchase or sale of any security registered on a national securities exchange or any security not so registered.” Not deception alone, but deception with respect to certain purchases or sales is necessary for a violation of the statute.</p>
<p id="b322-4">The Solicitor General points out that the “significant and material conduct” test is in accord with prevailing notions of international comity. If so, that proves that <em>if </em>the United States asserted prescriptive jurisdiction pursuant to the “significant and material conduct” test it would not violate customary international law; but it in no way tends to prove that that is what Congress has done.</p>
<p id="b322-5">Finally, the Solicitor General argues that the Commission has adopted an interpretation similar to the “significant and material conduct” test, and that we should defer to that. In the two adjudications the Solicitor General cites, however, the Commission did not purport to be providing its own interpretation of the statute, but relied on decisions of federal courts — mainly Court of Appeals decisions that in turn relied on the <em>Schoenbaum </em>and <em>Leasco </em>decisions of the Second Circuit that we discussed earlier. See <em>In re U S. Securities Clearing Corp., 52 </em>S. E. C. 92, 95, n. 14, 96, n. 16 (1994); <em>In re Robert F. Lynch, </em>Exchange Act Release No. 11737, 8 S. E. C. Docket 75, 77, 78, n. 15 (1975). We need “accept only those agency interpretations that are reasonable in light of the principles of construction courts normally employ.” <em>Aramco, </em><span class="citation" data-id="9432237"><a href="/opinion/112565/equal-employment-opportunity-commission-v-arabian-american-oil-co/#260" aria-description="Citation for case: Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Arabian...">499 U. S., at 260</a></span> (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). Since the Commission’s interpretations relied on cases we disapprove, which ignored or dis<page-number citation-index="1" label="273">*273</page-number>carded the presumption against extraterritoriality, we owe them no deference.</p>
<p id="b323-5">* * *</p>
<p id="b323-6">Section 10(b) reaches the use of a manipulative or deceptive device or contrivance only in connection with the purchase or sale of a security listed on an American stock exchange, and the purchase or sale of any other security in the United States. This case involves no securities listed on a domestic exchange, and all aspects of the purchases complained of by those petitioners who still have live claims occurred outside the United States. Petitioners have therefore failed to state a claim on which relief can be granted. We affirm the dismissal of petitioners’ complaint on this ground.</p>

Even if one thinks that the “conduct” and “effects” tests are numbered among Judge Friendly’s many fine contributions to the law, his successors, though perhaps under the impression that they nurture the same mighty oak, are in reality tending each its own botanically distinct tree. It is telling that the concurrence never attempts its own synthesis of the various balancing tests the Circuits have adopted.</p>
