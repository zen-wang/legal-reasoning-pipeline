9   II.  LEGAL STANDARDS                                                  
10        Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(f) permits a court to “strike from a pleading an 
11   insufficient defense or any redundant, immaterial, impertinent, or scandalous matter.”  Fed. R. 
12   Civ. P. 12(f).  “Immaterial matter is that which has no essential or important relationship to the 
13   claim for relief or the defenses being pleaded.”  Fantasy, Inc. v. Fogerty, 984 F.2d 1524, 1527 (9th 
14   Cir. 1993), rev’d on other grounds, 510 U.S. 517 (1994) (internal quotations omitted). 
15   III.  DISCUSSION                                                      
16        A.   Cain Declaration                                            
17        The inclusion of Dr. Cain’s Declaration and opinions in the TAC is procedurally improper.  
18   Affidavits and declarations “are not allowed as pleading exhibits unless they form the basis of the 
19   complaint.”  United States v. Ritchie, 342 F.3d 903, 907 (9th Cir. 2003).  And specifically in the 
20   context of securities fraud litigation, courts have stricken expert declarations that are attached to a 
21   complaint.   See In re Ashworth, Inc. Sec. Litig., No. 99CV0121-L(JAH), 2001 WL 37119391, at 
22   *3 (S.D. Cal. Dec. 3, 2001) (striking a declaration from an expert in the fields of statistics and 
23   applied mathematics as well as his legal conclusions cited in the complaint); City of Royal Oak 
24   Ret. Sys. v. Juniper Networks, Inc., No. 5:11-CV-04003-LHK, 2013 WL 2156358, at *7 (N.D. Cal 
25   May 17, 2013) (disregarding expert declaration attached to the complaint and the expert’s 
26   “opinions” copied into the complaint).                                
27        Although Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 10(c) permits the Court to treat a “written 
1   instrument that is an exhibit to a pleading” as part of the pleading for all purposes, an expert 
2   affidavit such as the Cain Declaration does not qualify as a “written instrument.”  In re Rigel 
3   Pharms., No. C 09-00546 JSW, 2010 WL 8816155, at *1 n. 1 (N.D. Cal. Aug. 24, 2010) (citing 
4   DeMarco v. Depotech Corp., 149 F. Supp. 2d 1212, 1220 (S.D. Cal. 2001) (A “written 
5   instrument” within the meaning of Rule 10(c) is “a document evidencing legal rights or duties or 
6   giving formal expression to a legal act or agreement, such as a deed, will, bond, lease, insurance 
7   policy or security agreement.”)).  An expert affidavit such as the Cain Declaration “is merely a 
8   piece of evidentiary matter that does not exist independently of the complaint,” and not a “written 
9   instrument” within the meaning of Rule 10(c).  DeMarco, 149 F. Supp. 2d at 1220; see also Stuart 
10   v. Cadbury Adams USA, LLC, No. CV 09-6295 AHM (CWX), 2010 WL 1407303, at *4 (C.D. 
11   Cal. Apr. 5, 2010), aff’d, 458 F. App’x 689 (9th Cir. 2011) (finding that an expert report in the 
12   complaint generated for the purposes of litigation is not a “written instrument” under Rule 10(c)); 
13   In re Herbalife, Ltd. Sec. Litig., No. CV142850DSFJCGX, 2015 WL 12734014, at *1 (C.D. Cal. 
14   July 28, 2015) (striking expert reports that “appear to have been generated for purposes of 
15   prosecuting this litigation” because they “do not constitute ‘written instrument[s]’ under Rule 
16   10(c)”); Thaut v. Hsieh, 2016 WL 3058235, at *10 (E.D. Cal. May 31, 2016), report and 
17   recommendation adopted, 2016 WL 10672012 (E.D. Cal. Aug. 11, 2016), aff’d sub nom. Edwards 
18   v. Hsieh, 745 F. App’x 6 (9th Cir. 2018) (striking expert declaration attached to complaint and 
19   disregarding same for purposes of considering motions to dismiss).    
20         Plaintiffs do not address or distinguish many of the cases cited above.  Instead, Plaintiffs 
21   rely on other cases, none of which the Court finds particularly helpful.  In MannKind, the court 
22   denied a motion to strike the expert report, reasoning that there exists “no inflexible rule” 
23   governing the sort of “written instruments” that may be attached to a pleading and that the expert 
24   report served merely to buttress Plaintiffs’ contentions.  In re MannKind Sec. Actions, 835 F. 
25   Supp. 2d 797, 821 (C.D. Cal. 2011).  In Cole, the court similarly denied the defendants’ motion to 
26   strike expert reports attached to the complaint, reasoning that the reports merely served to buttress 
27   the plaintiffs’ allegations of excessive force.  Cole v. Hunter, No. 3:13-CV-02719-O, 2014 WL 
1   266501, at *4 (N.D. Tex. Jan. 24, 2014).  To the extent MannKind and Cole suggest that expert 
2   reports are “written instruments” under Rule 10(c), the Court finds Ashworth, Royal Oak, and the 
3   other cases cited above to be more persuasive.2   Furthermore, in In re Enzymotec Sec. Litig., 2015 
4   WL 8784065, at *19 (D.N.J. Dec. 15, 2015), the defendants did not move to strike the expert 
5   declaration, and so the opinion does not mention, much less analyze, Rule 10(c).  Similarly, in In 
6   re Wash. Mut., Inc. Sec., Derivative & ERISA Litig., 694 F. Supp. 2d 1192, 1210 (W.D. Wash. 
7   2009), as well as in Nursing Home Pension Fund, Loc. 144 v. Oracle Corp., 380 F.3d 1226, 1234 
8   (9th Cir. 2004), the defendants did not raise any challenge to the experts.  Wash. Mut. and Nursing 
9   Home are also distinguishable from the present case in terms of the type of expert information 
10   being proffered.  In Wash Mut. and Nursing Home, the courts considered calculations prepared by 
11   the experts—data analysis regarding the home appraisal process in the former, and financial 
12   calculations in the latter.  In contrast, Plaintiffs in this case retained Dr. Cain to render opinions 
13   beyond merely data analysis.  Specifically, Plaintiffs rely on Dr. Cain to render opinions as to (1) 
14   whether the alleged misstatements and/or omissions would be expected to impact the investing 
15   decisions of a reasonable investor; and (2) proximate cause.          
16        B.   Portions of TAC That Cite or Rely on Dr. Cain’s Opinions    
17        Plaintiffs acknowledge in their Opposition that Dr. Cain’s Declaration “does not contain 
18   new facts or evidence,” and implicitly concede that it consists of opinions.  Opp’n at 5.  Dr. Cain’s

22   6217635, at *3 (N.D. Cal. Dec. 13, 2012) (striking from the complaint “conclusory assertions” 
23   from an expert report); In re Ashworth, Inc. Securities Litigation, 2001 WL 37119391, at *3 
24
