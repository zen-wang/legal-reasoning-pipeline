14        Currently pending before the Court is Plaintiff Glen Littleton’s motion for a temporary 
15   restraining order (“TRO”).  Mr. Littleton asks the Court to temporarily enjoin Defendant Elon 
16   Musk from                                                             

17             discussing this case and its underlying facts including but not 
              limited to: the character, credibility, or reputation of any party or 
18             witness; the contents of any pretrial materials or evidence in the 
              case; the strengths or weaknesses of the case of either party; and any 
19             other information Defendants or their counsel know or reasonably 
              should know is likely to be inadmissible as evidence and would 
20             create a substantial risk of prejudice if disclosed.        
21   Prop. Order at 1-2.  Mr. Littleton believes that a TRO is necessary based on recent comments 
22   made by Mr. Musk during a TED Talk held on April 14, 2022.  During the talk, Mr. Musk stated 
23   that funding had been secured to take Tesla private back in July-August 2018.  Mr. Musk made 
24   these comments after the Court issued its summary judgment order, which found that Mr. Musk’s 
25   tweets from August 2018 – claiming, e.g., that funding was secured – were in fact false and made 
26   with the requisite scienter.1

2   matter suitable for resolution without oral argument.  The motion for relief is hereby DENIED. 

3             The order requested by Defendant is a form of prior restraint: 
              “[c]ourt orders aimed at preventing or forbidding speech ‘are classic 
4             examples of prior restraints.’”  Marceaux v. Lafayette City-Par. 
              Consol. Gov't, 731 F.3d 488, 493 (5th Cir. 2013) (quoting Alexander 
5             v. United States, 509 U.S. 544, 550 (1993)).  “Prior restraints on 
              speech are disfavored and carry a heavy presumption of invalidity.”  
6             Greater Los Angeles Agency on Deafness, Inc. v. Cable News  
              Network, Inc., 742 F.3d 414, 430 (9th Cir. 2014) (quoting Long 
7             Beach Area Peace Network v. City of Long Beach, 574 F.3d 1011, 
              1023 (9th Cir. 2009)).  Due to the dangers they pose, prior restraints 
8             on speech are subject to strict scrutiny.  See Arizona Right to Life 
              Political Action Comm. v. Bayless, 320 F.3d 1002, 1008 (9th Cir. 
9             2003).                                                      
10   Doe v. Rose, No. CV-15-07503-MWF-JCx, 2016 U.S. Dist. LEXIS 203529, at *5 (C.D. Cal. Sept. 
11   30, 2016).                                                            
12        The Ninth Circuit has provided guidance as to when a prior restraint on trial participants is 
13   appropriate.  A court may issue a “gag order” against trial participants when “(1) the activity 
14   restrained poses either a clear and present danger or a serious and imminent threat to a protected 
15   competing interest; (2) the order is narrowly drawn; and (3) less restrictive alternatives are not 
16   available.”  Levine v. U.S. Dist. Ct., 764 F.2d 590, 595 (9th Cir. 1985). 
17        The Court finds that, in the instant case, Mr. Littleton has failed to make a satisfactory 
18   showing on any of the above elements.  For example, on (1), although this Court has a duty to 
19   insure the fairness of the trial and there will likely be a fair, or even substantial, amount of 
20   publicity at trial, the trial has now been rescheduled for January 2023.  See id. at 598 (indicating 
21   that publicity during or immediately before trial is more of a concern than “publicity months in 
22   advance of trial”); cf. id. at 600 (indicating that voir dire may eliminate bias caused by pretrial 
23   publicity).  Furthermore, the jury will be drawn from a “large, populous, metropolitan, and 
24   heterogeneous” community – a consideration the Ninth Circuit has stated is “critical” when a court 
25   considers “the likely effect of pretrial publicity on the jury pool.”  Dan Farr Prods. v. U.S. Dist. 
26   Ct., 874 F.3d 590, 594 (9th Cir. 2017).  Finally, it is worth noting that the substance of Mr. Musk’s 
27                                                                         
1   recent comments are consistent with the public positions that he has already taken during this 
2   litigation, including at summary judgment.                            
3        On (2), the proposed TRO appears overbroad in that it restricts Mr. Musk (not just his 
4   counsel) from speaking and that it prevents him from speaking to anyone (not just, e.g., media).2  
5   Cf. Levine, 764 F.2d at 593 n.1 (pointing out that only defense counsel and not the defendant 
6   himself was restricted from having contact with the media); id. at 595 (noting that “[t]he case for 
7   restraints on trial participants is especially strong with respect to attorneys”); Farr, 874 F.3d at 
8   593 n.3 (noting that, under Levine, “a lower standard applies to prior restraints of attorneys 
9   participating in a case, who are officers of the court subject to fiduciary and ethical obligations” – 
10   and that this lower standard “does not apply to non-attorney participants”) (emphasis in original).   
11        Finally, on (3) as indicated above, voir dire can address bias caused by pretrial publicity.  
12   See also Farr, 874 F.3d at 595 (noting that voir dire is not designed to exclude from the jury “all 
13   citizens who have read or heard about the case and who keep abreast of current events”; “rather, it 
14   screens out ‘those with fixed opinions as to guilt or innocence’”) (emphasis in original).  In 
15   addition, though “jury instructions are often an ineffective remedy,” Levine, 764 F.2d at 600, the 
16   Court finds that unlikely to be true for the instant case where the jury will be told that the Court 
17   has already found that the August 2018 tweets were false and made with the requisite scienter.  
18   See Farr, 874 F.3d at 593 (stating that “[a] prior restraint to ensure a fair trial is permissible ‘only 
19   if its absence would prevent securing twelve jurors who could, with proper judicial protection, 
20   render a verdict based only on the evidence admitted during trial’”); id. at 595 (adding that there is 
21   “a rebuttable presumption that juries follow jury instructions”).
